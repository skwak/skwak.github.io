2016 is upon us at full speed, and it's been a good year so far. I won [Algorithmia](http://www.algorithmia.com)'s [Shorties Contest](http://blog.algorithmia.com/2016/01/winners-of-the-algorithmia-shorties-contest-announced/), tacked another year/birthday to my age, and read some really great writing in the past 1.5 months — including [Sara Sutterlin's *Baveuse*](http://www.electriccereal.com/bookstore/baveuse-by-sara-sutterlin/) and Patti Smith's *Just Kids*. The former is a book of poems dripping with cruelty and beauty by Internet chum [Sara Sutterlin](http://sarasutterlin.com/) that you all should read if you like poetry (and sidenote: I'm in issue no. 1 of Sara's [Leste](http://lestemag.info/), which you should also read if you like poetry/art/writing/whatever). The latter book was a birthday present from friend and fellow Adie, [Loraine](http://www.lorainekv.com/).

*Just Kids* had been on my radar since it came out, but I never got around to reading it. The *idea* of it interested me, but I've always been a fiction-and-poetry person, and I worried I'd have to force myself to finish after 100 pages or so. I was totally wrong! I found myself staying up later each night just to get a few more pages in, and I ended up finishing the book late at night/early morning after I had officially gone to bed, unable to fall asleep thinking about what the ending might be like.

While I am sort of kicking myself that I just now read the book, probably reading the book this year, in 2016, after attending Ada, after landing my first junior developer role, was probably the most perfect time.

I have an arts background and worked in the non-profit visual arts, and I've been missing aspects of that life lately. I miss being around artists and the drive towards being weird for weird's sake. Those things *can* exist in technology, of course, but it's not the main drive. In a field known for its ever-flowing money (or, at least, stable incomes, which is not exactly what artists are known for), it's easy to get lost in stability, material goods, and keeping up with the Joneses or Kardashians.

Patti Smith and Robert Mapplethorpe, and the genre and discipline-bending approach and attitude to art, was a reminder and inspiration to keep true to my instincts and dreams and to *keep programming weird*.

When I first started programming, I read Paul Graham's *Hackers & Painters*, which I absolutely *hated* (If you're curious why, take a look at [Maciej Ceglowski's blog post](www.idlewords.com/2005/04/dabblers_and_blowhards.htm) about that silly book). I wanted to be inspired but instead felt like I was in a field (with a lot of exceptions, of course!) that mostly didn't understand, that my peers in my intersection of interests were pompous men in khaki pants who want to paint.

But why should my programming heroes have to be programmers? Aren't we all making things, figuring out our processes, and trying to carve a micro-niche into canvas/paper/keyboard/microphone/electronic slab for the future?

Some other things I learned from *Just Kids* and from Patti Smith, my programming hero:

- Good thrifted clothing and a unique haircut will take you farther than you might think.
- Poetry, music, and art (and programming?!) are pretty much kind of the same at the crux, just different social groups and different technical skills you have to work to get into.
- Blending disciplines together can be kind of hard because no one might get you for a really long time, if ever, but just keep going because all the elements are important to what you want to do, if you ever end up maneuvering into one disciplines over another, if you are the type of person to blend all disciplines together.
- Work without apology but make sure you have friends and supporters. So, motto: DGAF to anyone except your friends.
